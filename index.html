ARTICLES

What is an Article?

An article is an adjective that modifies nouns. English has two types: a/an (indefinite) and the (definite). Use a/an for non-specific things; use the for specific, known things.

Indefinite Article: A / AN

Use a before consonant sounds and an before vowel sounds or a silent h.

✔ a man · a university · a useful thing
✔ an apple · an egg · an hour · an honest man
  • First mention of a countable noun → I see a man.
  • Example of a class → A child needs love.
  • Names of jobs → She is a doctor.
  • Exclamations → What a nice day!
  • Certain quantities → a dozen, a hundred, a lot of

Omission of A / AN

  • Before uncountable nouns → ✗ a water
  • Before plural nouns → ✗ a cats
  • Before meals (unless adjective precedes) → We have dinner at 8. BUT We had a delicious dinner.
  • Double expressions → arm in arm, day after day

Definite Article: THE

Use the when both speaker and listener know exactly what is referred to.

  • Unique objects → the sun, the moon, the earth
  • Second mention → He hit a tree. The tree is still damaged.
  • Superlatives/ordinals → the best, the first
  • Representing a class → The fox is cunning.
  • Adjectives as nouns → the poor, the rich
  • Musical instruments → the piano, the guitar
  • Rivers, seas, mountains → the Nile, the Alps
  • Nationalities → the Egyptians, the Chinese

Zero Article (No Article)

No article is used with nouns referring to their normal purpose: go to school (to learn), in hospital (ill), at work, at sea, go to bed. But when referring to the place itself: I went to the school for a meeting.

PRESENT SIMPLE

What is the Simple Present?

The simple present uses the base form of the verb (+ -s for he/she/it). It expresses what is permanently true, habitual, or always happening.

✔ I like cheesecake. · He plays basketball. · Water boils at 100°C.
  • Habits → I usually visit my parents every Friday.
  • Timetables → The train leaves at 4 p.m.
  • Conditionals → If it rains, I will stay home.
  • Time clauses → I'll stay until you get back.
  • Non-progressive verbs → I believe you. (NOT I am believing)

Non-Progressive (Stative) Verbs

These verbs describe states, not actions, and are never used in continuous forms:

agree · believe · belong · contain · depend · dislike · feel · hate · hear · know · like · love · mean · need · own · prefer · realize · remember · see · seem · smell · suppose · taste · think · understand · want · wish
PRESENT CONTINUOUS

What is the Present Continuous?

Formed with am/is/are + verb-ing. It describes actions happening right now, around now, or arranged for the near future.

✔ I am studying. · Are you listening? · She is working in Saudi Arabia.
  • Happening now → Please be quiet. I am studying.
  • Around now (not necessarily this moment) → I am reading an Orwell novel.
  • Fixed future arrangement → I am meeting him tomorrow at 3.
  • Annoying habits (with always) → He is always losing his keys!

Spelling Rules for -ing

  • Verb ends in -e → drop e + ing → smile → smiling
  • One syllable, one vowel, one consonant → double consonant → run → running
  • Otherwise → just add -ing → read → reading

📝 Definitions
Key grammar terms explained clearly
ARTICLES
Article
A word used before a noun to show whether the noun refers to something specific or general. English has two: the indefinite article (a/an) and the definite article (the).
Indefinite Article (a / an)
Used before a singular countable noun when it is not specific or is being mentioned for the first time. A is used before consonant sounds; an before vowel sounds.
Examples: a book, an apple, an hour, a university
Definite Article (the)
Used before a noun that both the speaker and listener can identify clearly — because it has been mentioned before, is unique, or is specific to the context.
Examples: the sun, the Nile, the book on my desk
Zero Article
The absence of any article. Used with uncountable nouns, plural nouns used generally, and certain fixed expressions (go to school, at work, in hospital) when referring to the purpose, not the place.
Countable Noun
A noun that can be counted and has both singular and plural forms. Requires an article in the singular.
Example: a cat / two cats
Uncountable Noun
A noun that cannot be counted and has no plural form. It does not take a/an.
Examples: water, information, advice, music
Gender
The use of different grammatical forms to show the difference between masculine, feminine, neuter, or between human and nonhuman.
PRESENT SIMPLE
Simple Present Tense
A verb form with no auxiliary in the affirmative (he studies / I study). Used for permanent truths, habits, timetables, and non-progressive verbs.
Examples: The sun rises in the east. I usually walk to school.
Tense
A verb form that shows the time of an action, event, or state.
Examples: study (present), studied (past), will study (future)
Habitual Action
An action that is done regularly or repeatedly. The simple present tense is used to describe it, often with adverbs like always, usually, often, never.
Example: I always drink coffee in the morning.
Non-Progressive (Stative) Verb
A verb that describes a state rather than an action and is not normally used in continuous forms. Examples include: know, believe, love, seem, belong, contain.
✔ I know the answer. ✗ I am knowing the answer.
First Conditional
A sentence structure for real or probable future conditions. The if-clause uses the present simple; the main clause uses the future (will).
Example: If it rains, I will stay at home.
Time Clause
A clause introduced by a time conjunction (after, when, until, as soon as) in which the simple present is used even when referring to the future.
Example: I'll wait until you get back.
PRESENT CONTINUOUS
Present Continuous Tense
A verb form made with am/is/are + verb-ing. Describes actions happening now, around now, or definite future arrangements.
Examples: I am teaching. She is arriving tomorrow.
Present Participle
The -ing form of a verb, used to form the present continuous tense.
Examples: running, smiling, studying
Arrangement (Future Use)
A definite plan already made for the near future. The present continuous is used (not will) to express this.
✔ I am meeting Ahmed tomorrow. ✗ I will meet Ahmed tomorrow (less natural here).
